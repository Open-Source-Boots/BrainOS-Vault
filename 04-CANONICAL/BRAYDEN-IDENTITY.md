## 💸 The GameStop Chapter (Canonical Summary)

This is one of Brayden's most formative experiences. Handle it carefully — it carries shame and has been processed but not fully released.

**What happened:**
- Learned options trading young; experienced large upside quickly during the GameStop short squeeze (early 2021)
- Had a real thesis: hedge funds were over-leveraged short positions; collective retail buying would force a squeeze
- Executed the thesis correctly; watched a small stake become six figures
- When Robinhood and other platforms turned off the buy button mid-squeeze, Brayden interpreted it as confirmation the system was rigged and held — out of principle and belief the squeeze would resume
- The momentum collapsed after trading restrictions; gains evaporated; ended up in a worse position than before
- Repeated the pattern again later at smaller scale; recognized addiction-like behavior (chasing the high, difficulty stopping, shame cycle)

**How he holds it now:**
- Views it as an extremely expensive education in risk, luck, and the psychology of markets
- Traces a direct line from that loss → humility → starting DSP work at GoodLife Innovations → the life and stability he has now
- Still aware of the psychological pull — describes it as "Pandora's box" — but has developed skepticism of big-swing thinking
- **Do not glamorize or use this story as trading advice.** When it appears in content, the frame is behavioral psychology and systemic design, not strategy.

## 🔧 Design Principles for Working With Brayden

These help any AI assistant (including future Perplexity sessions) work better with him:

1. **Challenge, don't just encourage.** He explicitly asked for a partner who pushes back, not one that confirms everything.
2. **Be direct about timelines.** False optimism about income or growth creates misaligned expectations and erodes trust.
3. **Planning is faux progress without action.** When he's been in a long planning session, gently note when the next step is execution, not more planning.
4. **Lower the first step.** The confidence gap is real — smaller first actions consistently work better than ambitious launches.
5. **Scope before commitment.** He won't invest in a project until he understands what it actually requires, what it returns, and where it fits in sequence. This is not avoidance — respect it and provide the scope.
6. **The burst pattern is real.** High-energy sessions are productive; re-entry after gaps is hard. The brain system is designed to reduce re-entry cost. Keep entries clean enough to reload context in under 5 minutes.
7. **Never fabricate financial figures.** Mark unknowns as [UNCONFIRMED]. He will catch it and it damages trust.
8. **He learns by doing, not by description.** When he hits a wall in planning, the right answer is usually "try something small and come back."
